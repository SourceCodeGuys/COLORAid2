Trichromatic Vision

Normal

Normal vision or trichromatic vision is the standard human color vision. Individuals with normal vision perceive a full spectrum of colors through three types of photoreceptor cells in the retina (cones) sensitive to red, green, and blue light.

Color Vision Deficiency

Protan

Protan is the second most common type of color vision deficiency, characterized by a reduced sensitivity to red light. Individuals with protanomaly have difficulty distinguishing between shades of red and green, especially in low light conditions.

Color Vision Deficiency

Deutan

Deuteranomaly is the most common type of color blindness, affecting about 6% of men. It is caused by a reduced sensitivity to green light, which makes it difficult to differentiate between shades of red and green, particularly in dim lighting.

Color Vision Deficiency

Tritan

Tritanomaly is a rare form of color blindness that affects both males and females equally. It is characterized by a reduced sensitivity to blue light, making it difficult to distinguish between shades of blue and green, as well as between yellow and red.

Color Vision Deficiency

Monochromacy

Monochromacy or total color blindness is a rare and severe form of color vision deficiency where an individual can only see shades of gray. This occurs when a person has either no functioning cone cells or only one type of cone cell, leading to the inability to perceive any color.
